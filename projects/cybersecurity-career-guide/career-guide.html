The Reality of Breaking In

Cybersecurity is one of the most in-demand fields in technology, and one of the most frequently misrepresented to people trying to enter it. Bootcamp marketing and social media content routinely promise job-ready skills in weeks. The reality is different, and you deserve a straight account of it before investing your time and money.

Straight Talk

Reading this guide will not get you a job. Building knowledge, earning credentials, developing hands-on skills, and applying consistently over months to years will. This guide helps make that investment targeted rather than scattered.

What the investment actually looks like

Time: Entry-level certifications require meaningful study hours. CompTIA Security+ typically takes 60 to 100 hours of focused preparation for someone without a background. Beyond certs, building real skills through labs, CTFs, and home environments takes consistent time over months. The field moves fast enough that learning does not stop after the first job.

Money: Exam fees, lab subscriptions, and study materials add up. A realistic path to a first security role could cost anywhere from near-zero — leveraging free resources and the ISC2 CC — to several hundred dollars for exams and courses. This guide balances free and paid options throughout.

Self-direction: There is no teacher checking in. No class to attend. No deadline forcing you forward. The people who break into this field are the ones who build a consistent study habit without external accountability. If that is difficult for you, start small and build the habit before trying to build the knowledge.

The first role: Most people start at L1 SOC analyst, helpdesk-adjacent positions, or junior security roles that feel far from the work they actually want to do. That is not failure — that is the standard path. Experience compounds quickly in this field. Two years of L1 SOC work, done attentively, opens doors that no certification alone will.

The Upside

This field genuinely rewards people who are curious, self-directed, and willing to put in consistent work. It does not require a four-year degree in most roles. It does not require prior IT experience in all cases. The ceiling is high, the demand is real, and the work is meaningful. The investment is real, but so is the return.

Orienting Yourself

Before picking a certification or enrolling in a course, understand where you are starting from. The two tracks below are not rigid — they are starting points for orientation. Both tracks converge into the same gap analysis process described in Section 9. Read both if you are unsure where you fall.

// No IT Background

You are starting without prior experience in networking, systems administration, or development. That is a valid starting point. What it means practically is that security is unlikely to be your first role.

Target junior IT positions first — helpdesk, desktop support, NOC technician, or junior sysadmin roles. These build the environmental literacy that security work assumes. Use the gap analysis loop at every stage of that progression, not just when you are ready to move into security.

Once you have IT experience, your path toward security looks identical to the IT pivot track. The difference is the starting point, not the process.

  • CompTIA A+ — hardware, OS, and troubleshooting fundamentals
  • CompTIA Network+ — networking concepts security builds on
  • Professor Messer — free video courses for A+ and Network+
  • TryHackMe Pre-Security path — free, browser-based, beginner-friendly
  • Linux basics — most security tools run on Linux; get comfortable in a terminal
Go to Foundational Starting Points

// IT Professional Pivoting

You already work in IT — helpdesk, sysadmin, networking, development, or a related field. Your existing knowledge is more transferable than you may realize. The question is identifying which security domain maps to your current skill set and where the gaps are.

Your gap profile is narrower than someone starting from zero and your entry point into security roles is closer. The same gap analysis loop applies — your gaps will simply look different.

  • Networking → Network Security, SOC Operations
  • Sysadmin / Windows → DFIR, SOC, IAM
  • Linux / DevOps → Cloud Security, AppSec, DevSecOps
  • Development → Application Security, DevSecOps
  • Audit / Risk → GRC / Compliance, Data Security
  • SOC / IR experience → Cyber Threat Intelligence
  • Helpdesk → SOC Analyst (natural first step)

Using Job Postings as a Discovery Tool

Before you pick a direction, spend time reading real job postings. This is one of the most underused orientation tools available and it costs nothing. Job descriptions tell you what employers actually value right now — not what a curriculum vendor decided to teach two years ago.

How to Use This

Pull 10 to 15 postings for roles that sound interesting — even if you are not qualified yet. You are not applying; you are doing reconnaissance. What tools keep appearing? What certs are listed as required vs. preferred? The patterns across those postings are your actual target. Check LinkedIn Jobs, Indeed, and USAJobs for a broad sample.

01

Find Postings

Search LinkedIn, Indeed, and USAJobs for roles at the level you are targeting.

02

Identify Gaps

For each posting, list every requirement you currently do not meet: certs, tools, experience, knowledge areas, education.

03

Find Patterns

Across 10–15 postings, what gaps appear most frequently? Those are your highest-priority targets.

04

Build Your Plan

Use Sections 5 through 8 of this guide to find the certs, training, and resources that address your specific gaps.

05

Repeat

Run this again every few months. Your gaps will shrink. New gaps will surface. This is a habit, not a one-time task.

Breaking In — The Job Search Reality

Most guides stop at certifications. This section covers what actually gets you hired: what your resume needs to say, how to build evidence of skill when you have no job experience yet, what hiring managers at the entry level are actually evaluating, and where the market for security jobs lives.

What an entry-level security resume actually needs

Entry-level security hiring managers are not expecting work experience in security. They are evaluating whether you have the baseline knowledge and the demonstrated initiative to learn. A resume that shows certifications, hands-on platform activity, a home lab, or a personal project communicates more than a resume that lists job duties from an unrelated role.

Resume Reality

List every cert, including in-progress ones with an expected completion date. Include your TryHackMe or Hack The Box profiles if you have completed significant content. A GitHub with even a basic home lab writeup or CTF solution shows initiative. Tailor your resume to each posting using the job description language — the gap analysis method from Section 2 applies here directly.

Building a portfolio with no job experience

A home lab does not require expensive hardware. A laptop running VirtualBox or VMware with a Windows VM, a Kali Linux VM, and a vulnerable target like Metasploitable or DVWA is enough to start building real skills and documenting them. CTF competitions on platforms like PicoCTF, CTFtime, or Hack The Box provide structured challenges you can write up and post publicly.

For SOC-focused roles, building a home SIEM with a free tool like Wazuh or the ELK stack and documenting what you built is a concrete portfolio piece. For GRC-focused roles, a mock risk assessment or policy gap analysis against a public framework demonstrates the analytical skills the job requires.

What L1/L2 hiring managers actually look for

At the L1 level, managers are primarily evaluating coachability, attention to detail, and baseline technical literacy. They are not expecting you to have seen everything — they are evaluating whether you can be trained and whether you will ask the right questions. Know your resume cold. Be able to walk through your home lab or a CTF you completed in detail. Understand what the tools you list actually do.

At L2, demonstrated experience with specific tools — SIEM platforms, EDR consoles, forensic toolsets — matters more. Getting to L2 typically requires 1 to 3 years of L1 experience and evidence of growing independently within a role.

Understanding market rates

Compensation in cybersecurity varies significantly by geography, sector (federal vs. commercial), organization size, clearance requirements, and the specific role. Rather than reference salary ranges that go stale quickly, the most reliable approach is to check current rates through the sources below and cross-reference against real job postings in your target market. Many postings now include salary ranges directly — those are the most current and specific signal available to you.

Where to find security jobs

LinkedIn

The highest volume source for security job postings. Set up job alerts for your target role and location. Recruiters actively source candidates here — a complete profile with certs listed matters.

USAJobs

Federal government and contractor positions. Many federal security roles require or prefer Security+ and a clearance. Competitive compensation and strong job stability.

CyberSN

A niche job board focused specifically on cybersecurity roles. Less volume than LinkedIn but higher signal — the postings are security-specific and matching is more targeted.

Indeed

Broad coverage including smaller companies that do not always post to LinkedIn. Good for entry-level and mid-market roles.

Dice

Technology-focused job board with strong representation of security roles, particularly in consulting, contracting, and government-adjacent work.

MSSP / Consulting Firms Direct

Managed security service providers — CrowdStrike, Optiv, Secureworks, Booz Allen, Leidos — hire at volume for L1 roles. Check career pages directly.

Building relationships and attending events

Many first security roles come through a connection made at a local event, not through a job board. The cybersecurity community is more accessible than most people assume — professionals at every level show up to share knowledge, and most are willing to talk to newcomers who demonstrate genuine interest and initiative.

You do not need to attend expensive national conferences to build a network. Many of the most valuable community touchpoints are free or low-cost and happen locally or online. The goal is not to collect contacts — it is to become a familiar face in a community where people hire from within their network first.

Where to Start

BSides conferences are community-organized, usually free or under $25, and held in cities worldwide. They are the single best entry point for meeting working security professionals in a low-pressure setting. Find one near you at securitybsides.com. Local chapters of organizations like ISSA, OWASP, ISACA, and ISC2 hold regular meetups — most are free or included with membership. Online communities on Discord and Slack — including servers for TryHackMe, Hack The Box, and domain-specific groups — provide daily interaction with people working in the field. LinkedIn engagement with security practitioners — commenting thoughtfully on posts, sharing your learning journey, and connecting after meaningful interactions — builds visibility over time.

Show up consistently rather than once. Share what you are learning. Ask genuine questions. Offer to help with event logistics if you have no technical contributions yet. The relationships that lead to referrals and opportunities are built through repeated, low-key interactions — not through a single networking event.

The Disciplines

Cybersecurity is not one job. It is a collection of disciplines that share a common purpose but look very different day to day. Before picking a certification path, read through the domains below and ask honestly: does this sound like work I would find engaging every day? The right answer to that question is more important than which domain pays more or has the most job postings.

SOC Operations

Security Operations Center Analyst

SOC analysts monitor networks and systems for threats, investigate alerts, triage incidents, and escalate when something real is happening. At L1 you are working through an alert queue. At L2 and L3 you are doing deeper analysis, threat hunting, and leading incident response.

This role suits people who are methodical, comfortable with repetition that occasionally becomes urgency, and interested in understanding attacker behavior from a defensive position. It is the most common entry point into the security field.

Penetration Testing

Penetration Tester / Ethical Hacker

Penetration testers are hired to attack systems, networks, and applications before real attackers do. The work includes reconnaissance, exploitation, lateral movement, and reporting findings clearly enough that organizations can act on them.

This role suits people who think like attackers, enjoy puzzle-solving, have strong technical depth, and can communicate complex findings in writing. It is harder to break into than SOC roles and typically requires demonstrated technical skill over certifications alone.

GRC / Compliance

Governance, Risk & Compliance Analyst

GRC professionals ensure organizations understand their risk posture, meet regulatory requirements, and have the policies and controls in place to operate securely. The work involves risk assessments, audit preparation, policy writing, and framework alignment (NIST CSF, ISO 27001, SOC 2, PCI-DSS). GRC focuses on risk, policy, and compliance frameworks — distinct from Data Security, which focuses on the technical controls that protect data itself.

This role suits people who are detail-oriented, comfortable working across business units, and interested in the intersection of security and business strategy.

DFIR

Digital Forensics & Incident Response

DFIR analysts investigate security incidents after they occur — collecting evidence, reconstructing attack timelines, determining what was accessed or exfiltrated, and helping organizations recover. The forensics side involves disk, memory, and network artifact analysis. The IR side involves containing active threats and coordinating response.

This role suits people who are analytical, patient, and comfortable working under pressure during active incidents. Strong technical depth across operating systems, networking, and malware behavior is required at senior levels.

Cloud Security

Cloud Security Engineer

Cloud security engineers design, implement, and monitor security controls for cloud environments — primarily AWS, Azure, and GCP. The work covers identity and access management, network security groups, data protection, compliance automation, and cloud-native security tooling.

This role suits people with a strong foundation in cloud platforms who want to apply security thinking to infrastructure at scale. Prior sysadmin, DevOps, or cloud engineering background translates well here.

Application Security Engineer

AppSec engineers work at the intersection of development and security — reviewing code for vulnerabilities, integrating security into the SDLC, conducting application penetration tests, and helping development teams write secure code. The OWASP Top 10 is the foundational reference for this domain.

This role suits people with a software development background who want to move into security, or security professionals who want to understand how software is built and broken.

Network security engineers design and manage the security of an organization's network infrastructure — firewalls, IDS/IPS systems, VPNs, network segmentation, and traffic analysis. The work involves both architecture decisions and active monitoring of network activity.

IAM

Identity & Access Management Engineer

IAM engineers manage who has access to what — designing and implementing identity systems, single sign-on, multi-factor authentication, privileged access management, and directory services. As organizations adopt Zero Trust architectures, IAM has become a central discipline rather than an auxiliary one.

This role suits people who are methodical, interested in how systems authenticate and authorize users, and comfortable at the intersection of security and IT operations. Experience with Active Directory, Microsoft Entra ID (formerly Azure AD), or Okta is a common entry point.

Email Security

Email Security Specialist

Email security specialists configure and maintain secure email gateways, enforce authentication protocols (SPF, DKIM, DMARC), manage email filtering policies, and monitor for email-based threats. Critical findings are escalated to the SOC, security engineering, and IT teams. This is a preventive and detective role — not an incident response function.

Email remains the primary attack vector for most organizations. This role suits people who are detail-oriented and comfortable working across security and IT infrastructure teams. Dedicated vendor-neutral certifications for this domain are limited compared to other disciplines; foundational security credentials and platform knowledge carry more weight here.

Data Security

Data Security Engineer

Data security engineers implement and manage the technical controls that protect data at rest, in transit, and in use — DLP policies, data classification frameworks, encryption, database security, and privacy compliance. This is distinct from GRC, which focuses on risk and policy frameworks. Data security focuses on the technical mechanisms that enforce those frameworks.

The work spans regulatory compliance (GDPR, HIPAA, CCPA), DLP tooling, data governance architecture, and encryption key management. This role suits people who are systematic, technically inclined, and comfortable at the intersection of security engineering and compliance.

Cyber Threat Intelligence

Threat Intelligence Analyst

CTI analysts collect, analyze, and disseminate intelligence about threat actors, their tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), and the campaigns they run. The work involves tracking adversary groups, producing finished intelligence reports, developing detection signatures, and integrating threat data into defensive operations. MITRE ATT&CK is the foundational framework for this domain.

This role suits people who are analytical, strong writers, and comfortable working with ambiguous data to produce actionable conclusions. CTI sits between SOC operations, incident response, and strategic security leadership — feeding intelligence to all three. Prior SOC or IR experience provides valuable operational context.

Emerging & Cross-Cutting Areas

The disciplines above represent established career paths with defined cert stacks and job markets. The areas below are growing rapidly and do not yet have the same depth of dedicated certifications or standardized job descriptions — but they are increasingly present in job postings and organizational structures.

// DevSecOps

DevSecOps integrates security into the software development lifecycle and CI/CD pipelines rather than treating it as a post-build gate. Practitioners work with SAST/DAST tools, container security, infrastructure-as-code scanning, and automated compliance checks. This role sits at the intersection of Application Security and platform engineering.

Job postings increasingly list "DevSecOps Engineer" as a distinct title. Relevant backgrounds include software development, DevOps/SRE, or application security. Key tools include SonarQube, Snyk, Aqua Security, and CI/CD pipeline security integration. The Practical DevSecOps certification program is one of the few dedicated credentials in this area.

// AI & ML Security

As organizations adopt AI systems at scale, securing those systems has become a distinct discipline. AI security covers adversarial machine learning, LLM security (prompt injection, data poisoning, model extraction), AI red teaming, and AI governance and compliance. OWASP Top 10 for LLMs is the emerging foundational reference.

This area is evolving faster than certification bodies can keep up. The most relevant backgrounds are machine learning engineering, application security, or red teaming. Dedicated certifications are still emerging — current options include vendor-specific AI security training from Google, Microsoft, and NVIDIA, alongside the growing AI red teaming community.

Training Resources by Domain

MITRE ATT&CK Framework

The foundational knowledge base of adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures. Free, comprehensive, and non-negotiable for CTI work. Start with the ATT&CK matrices and work through the technique descriptions for your focus area.

SANS FOR578 — Cyber Threat Intelligence

The most respected CTI training course. Covers intelligence requirements, collection, analysis, and dissemination. Aligns to the GCTI certification. Expensive but authoritative.

Recorded Future University

Free training on threat intelligence fundamentals, analysis techniques, and the Recorded Future platform. Useful for learning how commercial CTI platforms operate regardless of which tool your organization uses.

FIRST TLP & CTI Standards

Free reference documentation for the Traffic Light Protocol (TLP), STIX/TAXII standards, and intelligence sharing frameworks. Understanding these standards is essential for anyone producing or consuming threat intelligence.

OpenCTI Platform

Free, open-source threat intelligence platform for structuring, storing, and visualizing CTI data. Building a home lab with OpenCTI and MISP provides hands-on experience with the tools used in production CTI programs.

Career Path Cert Stacks

Each path maps a three-tier certification progression from entry level through advanced. These are not the only valid certs for each path — they represent well-recognized, employer-valued choices that build on each other logically. Use the job posting gap analysis from Section 2 to validate which of these match what your target employers are actually asking for.

Not All Inclusive

The certifications listed here are a curated starting point for each path. Many additional valid certifications exist per domain. See the full Cert Reference Chart below and visit Paul Jerimy's Security Certification Roadmap for the original interactive view of certifications across all domains.

What the Tiers Mean

Entry Level — Attainable through self-study with no prior security role. These are the certs commonly listed as "required" or "preferred" in junior security job postings. They get your resume through initial screening.
Intermediate — Typically requires 1–3 years of hands-on domain experience to pass credibly. Listed as "required" in mid-level postings. These are career accelerators that signal competence beyond foundational knowledge.
Advanced — Requires deep domain experience (3–5+ years). Listed in senior and lead-level postings. These signal expertise and command salary premiums.

Quick-Start Checklist

The checklist below applies to both starting points — no IT background and IT professional pivoting. The process is identical. The gaps just look different depending on where you are starting from. Check items off as you complete them; the checkboxes are interactive for this session.

// No IT Background

Security is unlikely to be your first role. Target junior IT positions first — helpdesk, desktop support, NOC technician, or junior sysadmin. These build the environmental literacy security work assumes. Use the gap analysis loop below at every stage of that progression, not only when you are ready to move into security. Once you have IT experience, your process looks identical to Track B.

Target a junior IT role firstHelpdesk, desktop support, NOC technician, or junior sysadmin. Build real environment exposure before targeting security roles directly.
Build IT literacyCover networking basics (Network+), OS fundamentals (A+), and Linux command line. Use Professor Messer (free) and TryHackMe Pre-Security. A cert here is one option — hands-on experience in a junior IT role is another.
Run the gap analysis loopPull real job postings for the security roles you are working toward. Identify your current gaps across knowledge, experience, credentials, and education. This is the process — see the loop below.
Close the highest-priority gapsIf you are missing a cert that postings consistently require, get the cert. If you are missing hands-on experience, build a home lab or do CTF challenges. If you are missing knowledge, a course or self-study may be sufficient. Match the tool to the gap type.
Apply and debriefApply when you are close enough — not when you feel fully ready. Use every interview as a diagnostic. Questions that expose gaps you had not accounted for go back onto your gap list.
Repeat the loopContinue closing gaps, applying, and debriefing until you land the role. Once you are in, run the same loop for the next level.

// IT Professional Pivot

You already have IT experience. Your gap profile is narrower and your entry point into security roles is closer than someone starting from zero. The same gap analysis loop applies — your gaps will look different but the process is identical.

Map your existing skills to security domainsRead Section 5 honestly. Identify which security domains your current experience maps to most naturally. That is your highest-probability entry point.
Run the gap analysis loopPull real job postings for your target security role. List every security-specific requirement you do not currently meet — certs, tools, knowledge areas, experience, education. This is the process — see the loop below.
Close the highest-priority gapsA credential gap (a cert consistently listed as required) calls for a cert. A knowledge gap may be closeable through self-study or a course. An experience gap may be closeable through a home lab, a project, or platform work. Match the tool to the gap type.
Reframe your resumeExisting IT experience is security experience when framed correctly. Firewall management, patch work, access control administration, and incident tickets all belong on a security resume, written in security terms.
Apply and debriefApply to roles one level above pure entry if your IT experience warrants it. Use every interview as a diagnostic. Gaps surfaced during interviews go back onto your list.
Repeat the loopRe-run the gap analysis quarterly. Your target role will shift as your skills grow. The loop does not end — it scales with your career.

The Gap Analysis Loop — Applies to Both Tracks

This process works at every stage. Run it when you start, run it when you stall, and run it again when you are ready to level up.

01

Identify a Target Role

Browse 10 to 15 real job postings on LinkedIn, Indeed, or USAJobs. You are not applying yet — you are gathering intelligence about what employers actually need.

02

Audit Your Current State

For each posting, list every requirement you do not currently meet. Group them into four categories: knowledge gaps, experience gaps, credential gaps, and education gaps.

03

Choose How to Close Each Gap

See the gap type guide below. The right tool depends on the type of gap — not every gap needs a certification.

04

Close Highest-Priority Gaps First

What appears most frequently across your target postings is what to address first. Work the list consistently. Progress compounds.

05

Apply

Do not wait until you feel fully ready. Apply when you are close enough. The application and interview process generates information you cannot get any other way.

06

Debrief Every Interview

Whether you advance or not, identify what knowledge, experience, or credential questions exposed gaps you had not accounted for. Add those to your gap list. This is not failure — it is the most accurate gap analysis available.

07

Repeat

Continue closing gaps, applying, and debriefing until you land the role. Once you are in, run the same loop for the next level. This process does not end — it scales with your career.

Gap types and how to close them

Knowledge Gap

You lack understanding of a concept, technology, or domain area. Can be closed through a certification, a course, self-study, or hands-on practice — whichever fits your learning style and budget. A certification is one vehicle, not the only one.

Experience Gap

You have not done the thing hands-on. Can sometimes be closed through a certification with a practical component, a home lab, CTF participation, a personal project, or a role that builds the missing exposure. A cert that only tests knowledge does not close an experience gap.

Credential Gap

A specific certification is listed as required or strongly preferred in postings. This is one of the few cases where a certification is the direct answer rather than one option among several. Verify it appears consistently across postings before investing.

Education Gap

A degree is listed as required. Can sometimes be addressed through demonstrated equivalent experience, though this varies significantly by employer and sector. Federal and regulated environments tend to be stricter. Some roles will not move without a degree regardless of demonstrated skill.
